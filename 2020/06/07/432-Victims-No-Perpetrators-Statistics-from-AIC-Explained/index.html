432 Victims, No Perpetrators? Statistics from AIC Explained.

7 Jun 2020, 7:54PM | 218 words | 1 minute

Last Saturday, Australia saw tens-of-thousands of protestors take to the streets demanding justice for Indiginous Australians. This was inspired by the ongoing protests against police brutality and treatment of African-Americans in the USA. Shortly after demonstrations in the US emerged, similar evidence of mistreatment of Indiginous Australias went viral, resulting in a surge of outage. In particular this evidence was an assertion that over 400 Indiginous Australians have died in Police Custody since 1991, a statistic that has now been shared hundreds of thousands of times, widely accepted as true. On first glance, devoid of context this statistic seems pretty bad, a national tradegy to say the least.

What many people do not (or perhaps, don’t want to realise) is that this statistic has several caveats that significantly undermines the fundemental reason for outrage. In this post I will investigate this data in more detail, contrasting the results of research into custody deaths in Australia with the narrative that has been used to underpin mass-demonstrations across the country.

The Numbers don’t Lie
